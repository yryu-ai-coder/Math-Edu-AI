üìñ 1. Mean, Median, and Mode

These are three different ways to describe the "center" of a data set.

üìä Example Data Set: 2, 4, 4, 6, 8, 10

Mean (Average): (2+4+4+6+8+10) √∑ 6 = 34 √∑ 6 = 5.67

Median (Middle): 4, 6 ‚Üí (4+6) √∑ 2 = 5

Mode (Most Frequent): 4 (appears twice)

üìà 2. Data Distributions

Understanding how data is distributed helps us interpret patterns.

üìä Types of Distributions

  • Normal Distribution: Bell-shaped, symmetric around the mean
  • Skewed Distribution: Asymmetric, with a long tail on one side
  • Uniform Distribution: All values occur with equal frequency

üé≤ 3. Probability Basics

Probability measures the likelihood of an event occurring.

üéØ Probability Formula

P(event) = Number of favorable outcomes / Total number of possible outcomes

Probability ranges from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain).

üí° Example: Rolling a Die

What is the probability of rolling a 3 on a fair six-sided die?

P(rolling 3) = 1/6 ‚âà 0.167 or 16.7%

There is 1 favorable outcome (rolling a 3) out of 6 possible outcomes.

Problem 1: Mean Calculation

Find the mean of the numbers: 12, 15, 18, 20, 25

Answer: Mean = 18

Solution:

Mean = (12 + 15 + 18 + 20 + 25) √∑ 5

Mean = 90 √∑ 5 = 18

Problem 2: Probability

In a bag of 20 marbles, 5 are red, 8 are blue, and 7 are green. What is the probability of drawing a red marble?

Answer: P(red) = 1/4 or 25%

Solution:

P(red) = Number of red marbles / Total marbles

P(red) = 5/20 = 1/4 = 0.25 = 25%

Problem 3: Median

Find the median of: 3, 7, 2, 9, 1, 8, 4

Answer: Median = 4

Solution:

Ordered data: 1, 2, 3, 4, 7, 8, 9

Middle value (4th position): 4
